Chapter 9: Sample Workout Plans & Templates

Introduction

Practical application is key to transforming knowledge into results. Sample workout plans provide structure, consistency, and guidance for beginners and intermediates. These templates demonstrate how to organize exercises, sets, reps, and rest periods while incorporating all major muscle groups and training principles such as progressive overload, recovery, and balanced volume.

Beginner Full-Body Plan (3 Days/Week)

A full-body routine is ideal for beginners because it allows frequent practice of compound lifts while promoting balanced development. Start each session with 5–10 minutes of light cardio and mobility warm-up. Core exercises include squats, bench press, rows, overhead press, and deadlifts. Include accessory exercises for arms and core. Perform 2–3 sets of 8–12 reps per exercise, resting 60–90 seconds between sets. Adjust weights to maintain proper form while challenging muscles.

Intermediate Upper/Lower Split (4 Days/Week)

Intermediates can benefit from an upper/lower split to increase volume and frequency without overtraining. Upper-body days focus on chest, back, shoulders, and arms with compound lifts like bench press, pull-ups, and overhead press, followed by accessory movements. Lower-body days target quads, hamstrings, glutes, and calves with squats, lunges, deadlifts, and calf raises. Perform 3–5 sets of 6–12 reps depending on the exercise, resting 90–180 seconds for heavier lifts and 45–90 seconds for accessory work. Rotate exercises weekly to address weaknesses and prevent plateaus.

Push/Pull/Legs Split (5–6 Days/Week)

For more advanced intermediates, a push/pull/legs split allows focused attention on specific muscle groups multiple times per week. Push days target chest, shoulders, and triceps; pull days work back and biceps; leg days focus on quads, hamstrings, glutes, and calves. Include compound lifts first, followed by accessory exercises. Maintain 3–5 sets of 6–12 reps, adjusting load and rest as needed. This split supports higher training volume, progressive overload, and balanced development when managed correctly.

Tracking and Adjusting Plans

Using these sample plans as templates, track your progress by recording exercises, sets, reps, and weights. Adjust volume, intensity, or exercise selection based on progress, recovery, and goals. Gradually increase load or reps to maintain progressive overload. Customization and consistency are key: these plans provide structure but should evolve with your body and strength levels.
